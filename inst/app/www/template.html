Understanding synchrony-stability relationships in complex natural systems

Background and rationale

Ecosystems, like portfolios of financial assets, are complex hierarchical systems whose components fluctuate through time. These components include populations or communities across multiple spatial scales. For instance, we could think about how populations fluctuate locally within a community or about how communities fluctuate regionally within a metacommunity. Alternatively, species' populations fluctuating across a region form a metapopulation, and multiple metapopulations within a region together form a metacommunity.

As ecosystem components fluctuate, their dynamics tend to offset. This is called asynchrony, and results in the dynamics of higher-level ecosystem components being less variable over time than their component parts. For example, the temporal variability of metapopulation dynamics will be reduced relative to the dynamics of its component populations due to asynchrony. The reduction in variability at aggregate levels of complexity in an ecosystem is called the portfolio effect (PE). The magnitude of the PE scales with synchrony (φ), a measure of how correlated component dynamics are through time, where PE = 1 - φ.

In populations or communities subjected to resource harvesting, management strategies that conserve PEs are desirable as they function as "ecological insurance". In this sense, PEs may reduce variability in the extraction of resources through time. The purpose of this tool is to aid in the visualization and understanding of PEs as they manifest at different biological and organizational levels within ecosystems.

Usage

The functionality of {stable} reflects the idea that synchrony-stability relationships are general properties of complex systems. Therefore, it does not require that the user have data specific to ecosystem properites such as biomass or abundance. However, it is perhaps best suited to these types of data.
